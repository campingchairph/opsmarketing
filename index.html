Your role in campaigns

You execute, not decide. Every campaign action requires prior written approval from the Marketing Lead. Flawless execution and proactive status communication is your job.

Email campaign — 6-phase execution flow
1
Receive approved brief + content
Never start without written approval. Confirm: subject line, sender name, send time, list segment.
2
Build list in CRM / email platform
Upload contacts, apply segmentation. Check compliance — consent, opt-outs, financial services rules.
3
Set up email using approved template
Apply correct logo, disclaimer, formatting. No off-template design decisions.
4
Send test + QC checklist always
Broken links? Correct sender? Disclaimer visible? Rendering on mobile?
5
Get final sign-off before scheduling
Document approval (email or shared doc). Only then schedule.
6
Post-send: pull performance report
Open rate, clicks, bounces, unsubscribes. Add to tracker. Brief Marketing Lead on results.

Logo Rules — check every time
"Assetline Capital." — always with the full stop. The full stop is part of the refined wordmark. It is not a period you add yourself.
Single colour wordmark only. The orange dot in the wordmark was retired. If you see the old orange-dot version anywhere, flag it immediately and replace it.
Always download from the approved shared folder. Never use a personal copy, an old emailed version, or a screenshot. Re-download for every job.
Maintain clear space. The logo needs breathing room. Never crowd it against edges, copy, or other design elements.
Never stretch, rotate, skew, or add drop shadows, outlines, or effects. The proportions are fixed by the brand. If it doesn't fit the space, the layout needs to change — not the logo.
Never recreate the logo — even if you think you're "just replacing the font." Always use the approved vector file (.ai or .eps or .svg). A Word or PDF trace is not acceptable.
Never change the logo colour outside of approved light/dark variations. Orange is not a logo colour — it is the accent. The wordmark is single-colour.

Monday.com — your task hub

Monday.com is where your work lives. Think of it as a shared whiteboard your whole team can see. Your job: keep it accurate and up to date at all times.

What each view means
B
Board view
The main view. Rows = tasks, columns = status, owner, deadline, priority. This is where you'll spend most of your time updating task statuses.
K
Kanban view
Same tasks shown as cards in columns (To Do → In Progress → Done). Useful for seeing what's blocked at a glance.
G
Gantt / Timeline view
Tasks shown across a calendar. Use this to spot deadline clashes and flag them early to your Marketing Lead.
M
My Work view
Shows only tasks assigned to you across all boards. Check this first every morning — it's your personal task list.
Your Monday.com daily habits
1
Start of day — check "My Work"
See everything assigned to you. Sort by deadline. This is your task list for the day.
2
When you start a task — update status to "In Progress"
Never leave a task on "To Do" while you're working on it. Your lead needs to see real-time status.
3
When something is blocked — add an update comment
Don't just leave it. Click the task → Updates tab → write what's blocking you and tag your lead. This creates a paper trail.
4
When done — mark complete + attach the output file
Change status to Done. Attach the PDF, doc, or link directly in the task card. Never just say "done" in chat — attach proof.
5
End of day — check for overdue or at-risk tasks
Anything turning red (overdue)? Flag it to your lead before you log off. Never let a deadline pass silently.
The golden rule of Monday.com

If it's not updated in Monday, it doesn't exist. Your lead manages the team based on what they see in the board. An outdated status = they think you haven't started. Update it constantly, even when work feels obvious.

Remote first week — the real playbook

You won't be able to read the room, pick up office culture, or overhear how things work. You have to be more deliberate. This guide tells you exactly what to do each day.

Before Day 1 — do this at home
✓
Test your camera, mic, and internet
Do a test Zoom/Teams call with anyone. Bad audio on your first day is a bad first impression. Fix it before.
✓
Set up a clean, well-lit background
Natural light in front of you, not behind. Remove clutter. You're in financial services — professional matters.
✓
Install everything they tell you to install
Monday.com, Slack, Teams, VPN — get it all working before Day 1. Don't troubleshoot during onboarding calls.
✓
Prepare a notepad (physical or digital)
You will be overwhelmed with names, tools, processes, and acronyms. Write everything down. You'll need it in Week 2.
Day 1 — show up, absorb, ask one good question
→
Log on 5 minutes early
Remote first day: be in the waiting room before they are. It signals reliability immediately.
→
Your only job today: listen and take notes
Don't try to impress. Don't volunteer opinions. Ask clarifying questions about your tasks, not strategic suggestions.
→
Ask: "What does a good first week look like to you?"
Ask your Marketing Lead this on Day 1. It tells you exactly what they value and how they measure success. Most new starters never ask this.
→
Ask: "How do you prefer I communicate with you day to day?"
Slack? Email? Monday comments? How urgent is urgent? Remote teams run on communication norms — learn theirs immediately.
→
Write down every acronym or tool name you don't recognise
Look them up after the call. Never nod along and hope it becomes clear — it usually doesn't.
Days 2–3 — start building habits
→
Locate the brand asset folder — don't wait to be shown
Ask where logos, templates, and approved copy live. Get access. You can't do your job without this.
→
Find the approvals process — how does a task get signed off?
Who approves what? Where is it documented? If it's not documented, write it down as they explain it verbally.
→
Observe your lead's communication style and mirror it
Short and direct? Detailed updates? Emoji-friendly or formal? Match their energy in Slack/email until you know the culture.
→
Batch your questions — don't ask one at a time all day
Write down 3–5 questions, then send them in one Slack message. "Hey, I have a few quick questions when you have a moment" is gold.
Days 4–5 — prove you're reliable
→
Complete at least one task fully and without chasing
Pick the clearest task on your list. Finish it, QC it, update Monday, notify your lead. This one moment builds more trust than anything else.
→
Send a Friday EOW message to your lead
"Here's what I completed this week, here's what's in progress, here's what I need clarity on." 5 sentences. Use the template in the Templates tab.
→
Write down 3 things you're still unclear on
Not to ask immediately — to ask in your Week 2 check-in. Shows self-awareness and forward thinking.
The #1 remote new-starter mistake

Going quiet. In-office, your presence is visible. Remote, if you don't communicate, you're invisible. Over-communicate in Week 1 — update Monday constantly, send EOD summaries, respond quickly. You can dial it back once trust is built. You can't recover a "where are they?" reputation easily.
