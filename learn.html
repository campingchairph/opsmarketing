📧 Marketing Tasks

Email Marketing Platforms — What, Why & How

Understanding email marketing platforms like Salesforce is a core skill for any marketing coordinator. Here's what they are, why companies use them, and how they work.

ℹ️
What is an EDM? EDM stands for Electronic Direct Mail. It's a professionally designed HTML email — like a digital newsletter or product announcement — sent to a large list of contacts. Companies use EDMs to stay in front of their audience, promote products, and drive engagement.
What is an email marketing platform?
📧

Why companies use platforms like Salesforce

You can't send thousands of emails from Gmail
▼

Sending a marketing email to thousands of people requires a dedicated platform — not a regular email app. Email marketing platforms (like Salesforce, Mailchimp, Campaign Monitor, or HubSpot) exist because they:

  • 1
    Manage large contact lists

    They store thousands of contacts, segment them into groups, and let you target specific audiences with each send.

  • 2
    Handle HTML email templates

    Beautifully designed emails are built as HTML files. These platforms host and send them reliably across all email clients (Gmail, Outlook, Apple Mail).

  • 3
    Track performance

    Every open, click, bounce, and unsubscribe is recorded automatically. This data tells you how effective the campaign was.

  • 4
    Personalise at scale

    Merge fields like {{FirstName}} are automatically replaced with each contact's real data — so 10,000 people each get an email addressed to them personally.

  • 5
    Manage compliance

    Platforms automatically honour unsubscribe requests, maintain opt-out lists, and help companies comply with anti-spam laws.

How EDM sending works — the concept
🔄

The EDM workflow from brief to send

What a marketing coordinator does at each stage
▼
StageWhat happensWho does it
1. DesignA designer creates the email as an HTML file — layout, images, copy, linksDesigner / Manager
2. Template setupThe coordinator uploads the HTML into the platform, sets subject line, sender name, and sender emailCoordinator (you)
3. QC & previewCheck every link works, preview the email across devices, verify merge fields show correctlyCoordinator (you)
4. Test sendSend a test to yourself and/or your manager to review before it goes to the full listCoordinator (you)
5. ApprovalManager gives written sign-off that everything is correctManager
6. Schedule/SendSet the delivery time (converted to the correct timezone) and send or scheduleCoordinator (you)
7. MonitorWatch the send status — confirm delivery, flag any errorsCoordinator (you)
⚠️

QC — why it matters so much for EDMs

Mistakes go to thousands of people instantly
▼

Unlike a printed document you can pull off a shelf, an EDM can't be recalled once sent. A broken link, wrong phone number, or missing disclaimer reaches every contact on the list at once. This is why QC before sending is non-negotiable.

⚠️
Always QC before your manager reviews — your job is to send something that's ready. His job is sign-off, not error-finding.

Standard EDM QC checklist (applies at any company):

  • Subject line matches the approved brief exactly
  • Sender name and email address are correct
  • Every link in the email goes to the right destination
  • Merge fields show real data (not placeholder code) in the preview
  • All legal disclaimers are present and unedited
  • Images display correctly and are not broken
  • Send time is set to the correct timezone
Key concepts every coordinator must know
📋
Templates vs Sends

A template is the email design. A send is the actual delivery to a recipient list. You create a template first, then use it in a send. Publishing a template doesn't send to anyone.

👥
Recipient Lists

Contact lists in the platform. Each send is targeted to one or more lists. Choosing the right list is critical — wrong list = wrong audience.

🔀
Merge Fields

Personalisation codes like {{FirstName}} that get replaced with each contact's real data at send time. Always preview to make sure they're working.

⏰
Timezones

Send times must be converted to the platform's local timezone. A "9 AM Sydney" send might mean a different time where you're working — always confirm before scheduling.

Plain Text Outreach Emails — What & Why

Not all marketing emails are designed newsletters. Sometimes a simple, personal-looking email is more effective — and knowing when and why to use one is a real skill.

ℹ️
What is a plain text email? A plain text email has no HTML design, no images, no layout blocks — just words, like an email from a colleague. It's used when you want the message to feel personal and direct, rather than like a broadcast campaign.
EDM vs Plain Text — knowing the difference
EDM (Designed)Plain Text Outreach
Looks likeA newsletter or product announcement with images and layoutA personal email from a real person
PurposeBroad awareness, promotions, brand updatesDirect relationship-building, personal outreach
Sent byA company/brand addressAn individual person's name and email
FeelPolished, designed, broadcastCasual, personal, one-to-one
Your rolePrepare and send via the platformPrepare it — the named sender sends it themselves
How plain text outreach emails work
📝

Why companies use plain text for outreach

When personal beats polished
▼

When a senior person in a company wants to reach out to key contacts — brokers, clients, partners — a plain text email from their name feels more genuine than a designed newsletter. It's the digital equivalent of a handwritten note rather than a flyer.

As a coordinator, your job is to prepare these emails — writing or formatting the copy, setting up the send in the platform — so the named sender only has to review and approve. You do the behind-the-scenes work; they put their name on it.

💡
This is a common setup in many organisations. Executives don't set up emails themselves — that's what a coordinator does.
⚙️

The coordinator's role in plain text emails

What you're responsible for
▼
  • 1
    Receive the brief and copy

    Your manager or the sender provides the email copy (what to say) and the subject line. Your job is to set it up correctly in the platform.

  • 2
    Set up the email in the platform

    Enter the subject line and body copy. The sender's name, title, phone, and email are usually controlled by merge fields — you don't type them manually, the system pulls them from the sender's profile.

  • 3
    QC before handing over

    Check: subject line is correct, body copy matches the brief exactly, no spelling errors, merge fields are in place, nothing from the previous version was accidentally left in.

  • 4
    Notify the sender — they do the final send

    Once it's ready, you let the sender know. They review it and send it themselves using their own login. Your role ends when you say "it's ready."

    ✓ You prepare. They send.
Key principle
✉️

Never touch the sender's details

The sender's name, title, email, and phone number in a plain text email are tied to their personal profile via merge fields. You update the subject line and body copy only — never the signature block. Changing those fields could send incorrect contact information to every recipient.

EDM Reporting — Reading Campaign Performance

After every campaign, your manager needs data to evaluate how it performed. Your job is to gather that data, understand what it means, and present it clearly.

ℹ️
Why reporting matters Your manager can't make good marketing decisions without knowing what worked and what didn't. EDM reporting is how you give them that visibility — it's one of the most valuable things a coordinator can do.
The key metrics — what they mean and why managers care
📊

Campaign performance metrics explained

What each number tells you
▼
MetricWhat it isWhy your manager cares
Open Rate% of recipients who opened the emailTells you if the subject line was compelling enough to get attention
Click Rate% of recipients who clicked a link in the emailShows how engaging and relevant the content was
Bounce Rate% of emails that couldn't be deliveredFlags data quality issues — too many bounces means the contact list needs cleaning
UnsubscribesNumber of people who opted outA spike here signals the audience felt the content was irrelevant or they're being emailed too frequently
Click MapVisual showing which links in the email got the most clicksReveals what content people actually engaged with — useful for designing future campaigns
How to read the data and present it well
🗒️

Gathering and organising the report

What a good campaign report includes
▼

A well-structured EDM report gives your manager everything they need to evaluate performance at a glance. For each campaign, collect:

  • Campaign name — which EDM this is
  • Send date — when it went out
  • Subject line — what recipients saw in their inbox
  • Opens — number and percentage
  • Clicks — number and percentage
  • Bounces — number and type (hard vs soft)
  • Unsubscribes — number
  • Click map screenshot — visual of where people clicked
💡
All these numbers come from the platform's built-in analytics (e.g., the engagement report in Salesforce Account Engagement). You don't calculate them manually — you find them, record them, and present them clearly.
💬

What "good" looks like — industry benchmarks

So you can interpret the numbers, not just report them
▼
MetricIndustry averageGood result
Open rate20–25%30%+
Click rate2–4%5%+
Bounce rateUnder 2%Under 1%
Unsubscribe rateUnder 0.5%Under 0.2%

Knowing these benchmarks means you can flag when something is unusually high or low — not just hand over raw numbers, but actually contribute insight.

🗂️ Admin Tasks

Admin — File Organisation & Trackers

Good admin skills aren't about following a specific system — they're about understanding why organisation matters and applying those principles anywhere.

Why file organisation matters
📁

The purpose of structured file management

Why companies have strict file systems
▼

In a marketing team, multiple people touch the same files — the designer, the coordinator, the manager, and sometimes external suppliers. Without a clear, consistent file system:

  • People use outdated versions of files by mistake
  • Files get lost or duplicated across different folders
  • No one can tell which version is the "approved final" one
  • Recovering files during audits or compliance checks becomes impossible
💡
A good file system means anyone on the team can find what they need in 30 seconds, even if the person who created it is away. That's the standard to aim for.
File naming principles
✏️

What makes a good file name

Consistent, descriptive, dateable
▼

Good file names tell you three things at a glance: when it was created, what it is, and which version it is. Most professional environments use a date-first format (e.g., YYMMDD_Description) because files then sort chronologically automatically.

PrincipleWhy it matters
Include the dateFiles sort automatically by date — easy to find the most recent
Be descriptive"Email_Final" is useless. "ProductLaunch_BrokerEmail" tells you what it is
No spacesSpaces cause technical issues in many systems — use underscores
Version numbersAdd _V2, _V3 when revising — never overwrite the previous version
Never save to desktopFiles on personal desktops can't be accessed by teammates or recovered if your computer dies
Trackers — what they are and why teams use them
📋

What a tracker is and why it matters

The coordinator's central tool for staying organised
▼

A tracker is a shared spreadsheet (or document) that records the current status of ongoing work. Trackers exist because:

  • Your manager can't know what's happening across every task without asking — a tracker gives them live visibility
  • When multiple tasks run at once, it's easy to lose track without a central list
  • If you're out sick or on leave, someone else can pick up where you left off

Marketing coordinators typically maintain several types of trackers:

Tracker typeWhat it records
Campaign trackerEvery email campaign — who approved it, when it was sent, performance metrics
Event trackerUpcoming events, RSVPs, registration deadlines, venue bookings
Contact trackerKey contacts — brokers, suppliers, partners — and their status
Asset trackerDesign files, PDFs, approved versions — where they're stored and their status
⚠️
Update trackers immediately when a task changes — not at the end of the week. A tracker that's 3 days out of date is almost useless.
Professional communication principles
💬

How to communicate as a coordinator

Clear, proactive, and brief
▼

A marketing coordinator is the link between the manager, designers, suppliers, and stakeholders. How you communicate reflects on your professionalism. Key principles:

📣
Proactive, not reactive

Flag problems early — before a deadline, not after. "I'll miss Friday's deadline" sent Thursday is better than silence until Friday afternoon.

📝
Always in writing

Key approvals, changes, and decisions should be confirmed in writing — email or messaging app. Verbal alone is not enough.

✂️
Brief and clear

One paragraph is better than five. Get to the point: what happened, what you need, by when.

✅
Confirm deliverables

When you hand something over, say what it is, where to find it, and what action is needed. Don't leave people guessing.

Brand Guides & QC — What, Why & How

Every professional company has a brand guide. Understanding what it is, why it exists, and how to use it is a fundamental skill for anyone working in marketing.

What is a brand guide?
📘

What a brand guide contains and why it exists

Every company has one — you need to know how to use it
▼

A brand guide (also called a brand style guide or brand manual) is a document that defines exactly how a company's brand must look and sound — so that every piece of communication, no matter who created it, looks consistent.

It typically covers:

ElementWhat it specifies
LogoWhich version to use, which are retired, minimum sizes, spacing rules, what never to do with it
ColoursExact colour codes (CMYK for print, HEX/RGB for digital) — no approximating
TypographyWhich fonts are approved, which sizes, which weights, and when to use each
Tone of voiceHow the brand "speaks" — formal? Friendly? Direct? What words to avoid?
TemplatesApproved layouts for different materials — emails, brochures, social graphics
Photography styleWhat kinds of images fit the brand and what doesn't
Why companies have brand guides
🏢

The business reason behind brand consistency

It's not just about looking nice
▼
🧠
Recognition

When every touchpoint looks consistent, people recognise the brand instantly. Inconsistency confuses people and erodes trust.

⚖️
Compliance

In regulated industries (finance, healthcare, law), certain disclaimers and disclosures must appear exactly as written. The brand guide sets the non-negotiables.

👥
Team alignment

Multiple people produce materials — designers, coordinators, external agencies. The guide means everyone follows the same rules without needing to ask each time.

💼
Professionalism

A brand that looks consistent signals a company that has its act together. Mismatched fonts and colours signal the opposite.

How to check against a brand guide (QC)
✅

What QC means in practice

How a coordinator checks a file against the brand guide
▼

Quality Control (QC) means checking a finished design against the brand guide before it gets approved and distributed. As a coordinator, this is your responsibility — not the designer's.

💡
Think of QC as the last safety net before something goes public. Your job is to catch mistakes so your manager doesn't have to — and so the public never sees them.

Adobe InDesign — What It Is & How to Use It

InDesign is the industry-standard tool for laying out print and digital documents. You don't need to be a designer to use it — but you do need to understand what it does and how it works.

What is InDesign and what is it used for?
📐

InDesign's purpose and common use cases

Why this tool exists and what it's built for
▼

Adobe InDesign is a page layout application — it's used to arrange text, images, and graphics into professional documents. Unlike Word or PowerPoint, InDesign gives precise control over typography, colour, and print specifications.

What InDesign createsWhy InDesign instead of Word/PowerPoint
Brochures, flyers, rate cardsPrecise positioning, brand-controlled fonts and colours
Multi-page documents (reports, booklets)Master pages, automatic page numbers, consistent styling
Email templates and digital assetsCan export to both print-ready PDF and screen-optimised PDF
Event invitations and signageHandles bleed, crop marks, and print specifications automatically
✅
As a coordinator, you'll typically work with existing templates — placing copy and images into a pre-built layout, then exporting the final PDF. You don't need to build layouts from scratch.
Key concepts every beginner needs to know
🧠

InDesign fundamentals explained simply

The things that confuse beginners — demystified
▼
📦
Frames

Everything in InDesign sits inside a frame — a text frame holds text, an image frame holds images. You don't type or place directly on the page.

🔗
Linked Images

Images aren't embedded — they're linked from your computer. If you move or delete the image file, InDesign loses it. Always keep the Links folder with the file.

🔤
Paragraph Styles

Styles store all your text formatting (font, size, colour, spacing). Apply styles instead of formatting manually — it keeps everything consistent and on-brand.

🖨️
Bleed

For print, artwork extends 3mm beyond the trim edge (the "bleed"). This prevents white edges after cutting. You set this when you export the PDF.

✂️
Overset Text

When text doesn't fit in its frame, it's "overset" — hidden and won't print. A red + symbol on the frame means fix this before exporting.

🔍
Preflight

InDesign's built-in checker scans the file for errors before export — missing images, overset text, wrong colours. Always preflight before exporting.

The basic workflow for a beginner
🔄

From opening a file to exporting a PDF

The typical coordinator workflow
▼
  • 1
    Open the packaged folder

    A properly packaged InDesign job is a folder with the .indd file, a Links folder (images), and fonts. Always open from inside this folder — never move files around.

  • 2
    Check the Links panel

    Window → Links. Yellow warning = image modified. Red icon = image missing. Fix all warnings before making any edits.

  • 3
    Edit text using the Type tool

    Press T to switch to the Type tool. Click inside a text frame to edit. After pasting new text, apply the correct Paragraph Style — never format text manually.

  • 4
    Preflight before exporting

    Window → Output → Preflight. Green circle = no errors. Fix all errors before you export the PDF.

  • 5
    Export and verify

    File → Export → PDF. Use the correct preset (PDF/X-1a for print, High Quality Print for digital). Then open the exported PDF and scroll every page to confirm it looks right.

Adobe Illustrator — What It Is & How It Differs

Illustrator and InDesign are both Adobe tools, but they do different things. Understanding which tool does what — and why — is a key skill for anyone supporting a design team.

What is Illustrator and what is it used for?
🎨

Illustrator's purpose — vector graphics

Built for logos, icons, and single-image artwork
▼

Adobe Illustrator is a vector graphics application. It creates artwork made from mathematical lines and shapes — not pixels. This means the artwork can be scaled to any size (from a business card to a billboard) without losing quality.

What Illustrator createsWhy this tool specifically
Logos and brand iconsLogos must scale perfectly — only vectors do this
Social media graphics, bannersSingle artboard, precise layouts, flexible export
Charts, infographics, diagramsClean lines, editable shapes, brand colours
Print artwork (single page)High-precision output with CMYK colour support
ℹ️
Illustrator vs InDesign in simple terms: Illustrator = one graphic or artwork. InDesign = multi-page documents with many elements. A logo is made in Illustrator; a brochure is laid out in InDesign, using that logo.
Key concepts for a beginner
🧠

What you need to understand before touching Illustrator

The foundational ideas
▼
📐
Vector vs Pixel

Vectors are infinitely scalable — no blur or pixelation. Raster/pixel images (JPG, PNG) lose quality when enlarged. Logos must always be vectors.

🗂️
Artboard

The working canvas in Illustrator. Like a page in InDesign. You design within the artboard boundaries. A file can have multiple artboards for different sizes.

🎨
Swatches

Saved brand colours. Always colour from approved swatches — never use the freehand colour picker. This keeps brand colours consistent across every file.

🖨️
CMYK vs RGB

Print files must use CMYK. Screen/digital files use RGB. Using the wrong mode means colours look different in the final output than on your screen.

How to use Illustrator as a beginner coordinator
🔄

Typical tasks and how to approach them

What you'll actually do day-to-day
▼
  • 1
    Open an existing file — never create from scratch

    As a coordinator, your job is to edit existing artwork, not design new artwork. Open the approved template or artwork file provided by your designer or from the shared folder.

  • 2
    Edit text with the Type tool (T)

    Press T, click into a text block, and edit. Never change the font or colour manually — match what's already there. If text shows in an unexpected font (pink highlight = font missing), use Type → Find/Replace Font to fix it.

  • 3
    Resize images proportionally

    When resizing anything, hold Shift to maintain proportions. Stretching a logo or graphic non-proportionally is a brand error.

  • 4
    Export for the right output

    Screen/web/email: Export As → PNG (RGB). Print: Save As → PDF (CMYK, PDF/X-1a preset). Social media: Export for Screens → PNG/JPG.

  • 5
    Run spell check before export

    Edit → Spelling → Check Spelling. Easy to miss typos on a banner or social graphic. Always check before handing over.

PDFs & Adobe Acrobat — What, Why & How

PDFs are the standard format for sharing and distributing finished documents. Knowing how to review, annotate, and make minor edits in Acrobat is an essential coordinator skill.

What is a PDF and why is it the standard?
📄

Why everyone uses PDF for final documents

It's not just a file format — it's a guarantee
▼

PDF (Portable Document Format) was created specifically so that a document looks identical on any device, any operating system, any screen. A Word document can reflow text or show different fonts depending on who opens it. A PDF does not — it's locked.

PDF advantageWhy it matters
Looks the same everywhereWhat you design is what the client sees — fonts, layout, colours, all preserved
Non-editable by defaultRecipients can't accidentally (or intentionally) change the content
Print-readyPrint shops require PDFs — they include crop marks, bleed, and colour profiles
UniversalOpens on every device without special software
What Adobe Acrobat does
🔧

Acrobat's role — review, annotate, minor edit

It's not a design tool
▼

Adobe Acrobat is the professional application for working with PDFs. It's for reviewing and annotating — not for redesigning. Major changes always go back to the original InDesign or Illustrator file.

✅
What Acrobat is for

Opening and reviewing PDFs properly. Adding comments/annotations for corrections. Making tiny text edits (a wrong date, a typo). Checking print specifications.

❌
What Acrobat is NOT for

Layout changes, font changes, image swaps, colour changes, or anything that alters the design. Those go back to InDesign/Illustrator.

How to review and annotate a PDF
🔍

Reviewing a PDF — the right way

Never skim — every page, every element
▼
  • 1
    Open in Acrobat — not a browser viewer

    Right-click → Open with → Adobe Acrobat. Browser previews miss issues. Acrobat shows everything correctly.

  • 2
    Scroll every page at 100% zoom

    Set zoom to 100% (Ctrl+1). Go page by page. Check: layout looks right, no cut-off text, all images present, no blank pages, correct page order.

  • 3
    Check all small text — disclaimers, footnotes, dates

    Zoom in (Ctrl+=) and read the fine print. Disclaimers must be present and accurate. Wrong dates or figures in small text are easy to miss.

  • 4
    Use comment tools to mark corrections

    Tools → Comment. Use sticky notes to explain what's wrong. Highlight text that needs changing. Be specific: "Change 'from' to 'form'" not just "fix this". Send the annotated PDF back — corrections happen in InDesign.

✏️

Minor text edits in Acrobat

Only for very small, approved fixes
▼

If a tiny fix is needed and going back to InDesign isn't practical (e.g., changing a phone number in a finalised document), Acrobat can edit text directly.

How: Tools → Edit PDF → Edit. Click the text block to enter edit mode. Make the change. Save a copy with a new filename so the original is preserved.

⚠️
Acrobat may not have the exact font installed — it may substitute it, causing the text to look different. Use this only for minor emergency fixes. Always tell your manager what was changed and why.
